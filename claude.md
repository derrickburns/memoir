## Methodology: Through-Line Analysis

### Rating Scale (1-5)

**5 - Essential (Load-bearing):**
- Advances multiple through-lines
- Creates emotional impact
- Establishes or shifts patterns
- Cannot be removed without breaking narrative

**4 - Highly Valuable:**
- Clearly advances at least one through-line
- Provides necessary context or emotional texture
- Could be compressed slightly but shouldn't be removed

**3 - Valuable but Compressible:**
- Supports through-lines but could be tighter
- Provides color but isn't essential
- First candidate for compression if chapter is too long

**2 - Marginal:**
- Unclear connection to through-lines
- Might be important if author clarifies
- Requires questions before deciding to keep/cut/strengthen

**1 - Expendable:**
- Doesn't advance through-lines
- Sensory tourism without emotional stakes
- Redundant demonstrations of already-established patterns
- Should be cut

### 9 Patterns in Low-Value Paragraphs (Cut These)

1. **Sensory Tourism Without Emotional Stakes** - "Church shoes squeaking" without meaning
2. **Character Introductions Leading Nowhere** - People who never return or matter
3. **Extended Procedural Sequences** - Step-by-step descriptions without emotional weight
4. **Redundant Demonstrations** - Showing the same pattern multiple times
5. **Documentary Evidence** - Including full reports when excerpts would work
6. **False Starts** - Beginning scenes that don't develop
7. **Explanatory Digressions** - Over-explaining what the reader can infer
8. **Comparison Stating vs. Showing** - Telling differences instead of showing them
9. **Information-Only Dialogue** - Conversations that only convey facts

### Analysis Process (Step-by-Step)

1. **Read the entire chapter** to understand its arc
2. **Identify through-lines present** in this chapter
3. **Rate each paragraph** (1-5 scale) based on through-line advancement
4. **Identify expendable content** (Rating 1) - recommend cutting
5. **Identify marginal content** (Rating 2) - ask author questions to clarify
6. **Ask specific questions** about marginal content:
   - Is this memory real or speculation?
   - Does this detail serve a purpose later?
   - What is this scene meant to show/establish?
7. **Create strengthened version** based on author's answers
8. **Apply author's editorial judgment** - they often have a better instinct than your first analysis

### Key Principles

**Show Don't Tell (Usually):**
- Remove over-explanation after scenes
- Trust reader to synthesize patterns
- BUT: Memoir can include adult reflection‚Äîuse sparingly

**Character Economy:**
- Only include people who advance through-lines
- If someone appears once and never returns, cut them unless they establish a pattern

**Specificity Over Generality:**
- "I rocked myself to sleep for years" > "I felt upset"
- Specific details that matter > Generic descriptions

**Honesty Over Drama:**
- Real memories > invented scenes
- Acknowledged unknowing > speculation
- "I don't know why" is powerful when true

**The Alternative Version Test:**
- Author may propose alternative versions
- Compare systematically: what does each version accomplish?
- Author's instinct about what "feels right" often reveals what matters most

**Who Derrick Is:**

- **Deep psychological insight** - writing to understand his own mind and the minds of others

**What This Means for Editing:**

### Voice Requirements:

1. **Direct, Not Evasive**
   - Cut euphemisms that soften hard truths
   - Keep language that confronts pain directly
   - Avoid "literary" flourishes that distance the reader from raw experience
   - Example: "Mom's control" not "Mom's well-intentioned guidance"

2. **Psychological Depth, Not Surface**
   - This memoir seeks deep insight into patterns, not just recounting events
   - Keep moments of psychological revelation
   - Cut purely descriptive passages that don't reveal inner truth
   - The "why" matters as much as the "what"

3. **Raw and Authentic, Not Polished Away**
   - Literary = elevated language that serves truth
   - Literary ‚â† abstract, distanced, or overly aesthetic
   - Keep the jagged edges, the contradictions, the uncomfortable truths
   - Example: "I rocked myself to sleep for years" is both literary AND raw

4. **Black Experience, Not White Gaze**
   - This is NOT a story designed to make white readers comfortable
   - Avoid tropes of the "inspirational Black narrative"
   - No "magical Negro" moments, no salvation-through-suffering narrative
   - Keep the specificity of Black Detroit, Black church, navigating white institutions
   - Racial dynamics are named directly, not euphemized
   - Example: The segregated Army photo is confronted, not softened

5. **Truth That Smacks the Reader**
   - Don't smooth over contradictions (loving Mom AND feeling controlled)
   - Don't explain away complexity (Aunt Mae's love vs. Mom's love - both real, both valid, both different)
   - Keep moments that make the reader uncomfortable (group home threat, emotional shutdown)
   - Trust discomfort - it means truth is landing

### Red Flags That Violate Voice:

‚ùå **Over-explaining to comfort the reader**
   - "Of course Mom loved me, she just..." ‚Üí NO
   - Let complexity stand without justification

‚ùå **Abstraction that distances from pain**
   - "I felt a sense of disconnection" ‚Üí NO
   - "I rocked myself to sleep for years" ‚Üí YES

‚ùå **Tropes of Black struggle**
   - Avoid: "Despite the odds..." / "He overcame..." / "Against all obstacles..."
   - This is not an "overcoming" narrative - it's a truth-seeking narrative

‚ùå **Literary language that prioritizes beauty over truth**
   - Avoid: Overly poetic descriptions that obscure rather than reveal
   - Keep: Precise, elevated language that cuts deeper into truth

‚ùå **Softening hard truths**
   - Mom's conditional love is CONDITIONAL - don't soften it
   - Emotional shutdown is SHUTDOWN - don't romanticize it as "self-protection"
   - Racism is RACISM - name it directly

## Chapter Quality Checklist

### 1. Weak Character Development
**‚ùå Problem:** Characters introduced but not developed, or development that doesn't serve the arc

**‚ùå Problem:** Details included because they're memorable, not because they advance the story
**‚úÖ Solution:**
- Details must serve emotional truth or through-line advancement
- "Interesting" ‚â† "necessary"

### 3. Too Much Telling, Not Enough Showing
**‚ùå Problem:** Explaining what happened instead of letting the reader experience it
**‚úÖ Solution:**
- Show through concrete scenes with dialogue and action
- Tell only when compression is essential
- Cut adult reflective analysis that explains what scenes already showed
- Ask: "Am I showing the reader what happened, or explaining to them what it meant?"

### 4. Concepts Introduced Without Proper Context
**‚ùå Problem:** References, terms, or ideas appear without the reader having necessary background
**‚úÖ Solution:**
- Introduce concepts with enough context for reader to understand
- Don't assume reader knows what "The Project" or "conditional love" means
- Ground abstract concepts in concrete examples FIRST
- Ask: "Would a reader who knows nothing about this understand what I'm referring to?"

**Each option should:**

4. Include brief explanation of what each option accomplishes

