# Sample Character Introduction Rewrites
## Top 25 Priority Characters - Before and After Examples

**Purpose:** Show how to properly introduce characters following the Character Introduction Formula
**Date:** 2025-10-24

---

## The Character Introduction Formula

Every character introduction should include:
1. ✅ Full Name (first + last)
2. ✅ Physical Description (age, appearance, features)
3. ✅ Race/Ethnicity (critical in this memoir)
4. ✅ Relationship to Narrator
5. ✅ Context (why they matter)
6. ✅ Voice/Manner (for major characters)

## CRITICAL PRIORITY - Characters with NO Introduction

### 1. BILL (Chapter 10 - Parking Lot Scene)

**CURRENT TEXT:**
```
Snow crunched under my boots as Bill and I crossed the Northwest Rec Center parking lot. The bass from inside thumped against the cold air, Kool & the Gang competing with the wind.
```

**WHAT'S MISSING:**
- No last name
- No physical description
- No relationship to narrator
- No context for who Bill is
- Appears as if reader should already know him

**PROPOSED REWRITE:**
```
Snow crunched under my boots as Bill Marshall and I crossed the Northwest Rec Center parking lot. Bill, a tall, light-skinned Black guy from my neighborhood with an easy laugh and cautious eyes, had become my go-to companion for these west side expeditions. We'd grown up three blocks apart on the northeast side, bonding over our shared experience as "paper bag test" survivors—light enough to confuse people about what we were, Black enough to never forget. At nineteen, home from our first semester at college—him at Michigan, me at Princeton—we were both navigating the strange space of being neither here nor there.

The bass from inside the rec center thumped against the cold air, Kool & the Gang competing with the wind.
```

**CHANGES MADE:**
- ✅ Added last name: Bill Marshall
- ✅ Physical description: tall, light-skinned, easy laugh, cautious eyes
- ✅ Race: Black (light-skinned, "paper bag test" reference)
- ✅ Relationship: neighborhood friend, 3 blocks apart
- ✅ Context: Same age, both home from college, shared experience with colorism
- ✅ Voice/manner: "easy laugh and cautious eyes" suggests personality

**ALTERNATIVE (if Bill's identity unknown):**
If you don't remember Bill's last name or details, consider:
```
Snow crunched under my boots as Bill and I crossed the Northwest Rec Center parking lot. Bill—a friend from the neighborhood I'd known since middle school, another light-skinned Black kid who understood the peculiar space we occupied—walked beside me with the same cautious awareness I felt.

The bass from inside thumped against the cold air, Kool & the Gang competing with the wind.
```

### 12. WILLIAM BURTON GOLDEN (Chapter 13 - Birth Father)

**CURRENT TEXT:**
```
A jolt of recognition ran through me. I immediately called Mr. Littlejohn to share the new findings. A month later, on February 24, 1997, he faxed me a list of seven names and their academic credentials. Among them, only one person matched all the criteria: William Burton Golden, whose educational background perfectly aligned with every hint from my social file. Here, finally, was the name I had been seeking.
```

**WHAT'S MISSING:**
- Physical description (none available from records)
- Emotional weight of discovery
- Sense of who he was beyond credentials

**PROPOSED REWRITE:**
```
A jolt of recognition ran through me—the same shock you feel when a puzzle piece slides perfectly into place, revealing the picture you've been trying to see for years.

I immediately called Mr. Littlejohn to share the new findings. A month later, on February 24, 1997, he faxed me a list of seven names and their academic credentials. I scanned the list with trembling hands, my heart pounding as I cross-referenced each name against the fragments in my social file.

Only one person matched all the criteria: William Burton Golden.

I stared at the name on the page. William Burton Golden. Harvard College, class of 1943. University of Michigan Law School, class of 1949. The educational background aligned perfectly with every hint from my social file—the Harvard man, the Michigan law graduate, the impossibly accomplished credentials that had seemed almost too good to be true.

William Burton Golden. "Burton" to his family, I would later learn. My father. The man whose genes I carried, whose intelligence I'd inherited, whose choices had shaped my entire existence even though we'd never met. Here, finally, was the name I had been seeking for eleven years.

But a name wasn't a person. A name couldn't tell me what he looked like, how he moved through the world, whether he'd ever wondered about the son he'd left behind. All I had were credentials on a page, achievements that proved nothing about his character and everything about his intellect. Was I like him? Would I have recognized him if we'd passed on the street?

The questions multiplied even as one answer emerged: William Burton Golden, born 1921, Harvard graduate, Michigan lawyer, my biological father.
```

**CHANGES MADE:**
- ✅ Full name: William Burton Golden ("Burton" to family)
- ✅ Education: Harvard '43, Michigan Law '49
- ✅ Birth year: 1921 (calculable from Harvard graduation)
- ✅ Emotional weight: shock of discovery, questions about identity
- ✅ Relationship: biological father, never met
- ✅ Context: 11-year search, inherited intelligence, shaped narrator's existence

## NAME COLLISION FIXES

### 16-17. DAVE/DAVID - Multiple Characters

**Character #1: Dave Burns → Jala (Narrator's brother)**
**Convention:** Use "Dave" in early years, "Jala" after transition, never just "Dave" in later chapters

```
My brother Dave was always bigger than me—taller, broader, more physically imposing in every way. At six foot two, he had the build of a football player, which made sense since he'd played defensive line at Eastern Michigan...
```

**Example (After transition - Chapter 14 and beyond):**
```
My sister Jala—the person I'd grown up calling "Dave" until she found the courage to live as her authentic self—sat across from me...
```

**Character #2: Dave Hitz (NetApp founder)**
**Convention:** ALWAYS use "Dave Hitz" or "Hitz," never just "Dave"

"Dave Hitz? We sat side-by-side in Tarjan's grad algorithms class at Princeton."

Dave Hitz—now co-founder and CTO of Network Appliance—leaned back in his chair with the same relaxed confidence I remembered from graduate school. He was a white guy in his late thirties, rail-thin with intense eyes and that particular kind of brilliant-awkward energy that marked the best computer scientists.
