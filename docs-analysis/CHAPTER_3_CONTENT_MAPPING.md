### Scene 3: Boys Town

**Finalized Chapter 3 (lines 325-347):**

```
**Boys Town**

One summer, we took a family road trip toward Los Angeles. Dad had the AAA Triptik
mapped out, the TourBook marking potential stops. Dave and I fought over who got the
window seat until Mom made us switch every hour.

Mom had planned a detour: Boys Town in Nebraska.

We pulled into the parking lot, and there it was—the bronze statue that appeared on
every piece of Boys Town literature. A bigger boy carrying a smaller one on his back,
the inscription reading: "He ain't heavy, Mister... he's m' brother!"

Families gathered around, taking photos. A little kid asked his mother, "Why's that
boy carrying the other one?"
"Because that's what brothers do," she said. "They help each other."

Dave's face went expressionless—almost like he was asleep except his eyes were wide
open. I felt my pulse racing, pressure building in my chest, a lump forming in my
throat I couldn't swallow.

Mom's hand rested on Dave's shoulder as the tour guide talked about Father Flanagan,
about how Boys Town started as a place for boys who had nowhere else to go. Boys who
needed homes.

Boys like Dave?

I couldn't meet Dave's eyes. Questions I couldn't ask hung in the air between us: Did
Dave think this was why we stopped here? Was Mom reconsidering? Was she showing us
what could still happen if we didn't behave?

The drive away from Boys Town was subdued... Then I felt Dave's hand find mine between
us on the back seat—a simple gesture that carried everything we couldn't say aloud.

We never made it to Los Angeles that trip. Dad was too tired, and he wouldn't let Mom
drive. Denver became our destination...
```

## Additional Context: Why These Scenes Were Pulled Forward

### Strategic Reasons:

1. **Dave at Aunt Mae's** - Shows unconditional love applies to both boys
   - Prevents reader from thinking Aunt Mae just preferred Derrick
   - Establishes pattern early (unconditional love is universal, not earned)

2. **Dad's Bowling Alley** - Shows Dad's need for escape parallels Derrick's
   - Creates thematic unity (everyone needs refuge from Mom's intensity)
   - Shows family system, not just individual experience
   - Adds Dad characterization early

3. **Boys Town** - Establishes adoption insecurity stakes early
   - Foreshadows Dave's later arc
   - Shows conditional belonging threat
   - Creates emotional weight for both boys' experience

### Narrative Benefits:

- **Tighter childhood arc** - All key patterns established in first 5 chapters
- **Better pacing** - Adult chapters (12+) can reference established patterns
- **Clearer through-lines** - Unconditional vs. conditional love fully demonstrated
- **Stronger emotional core** - Family dynamics visible early
