## CHAPTER 1: Two Worlds Converging

**Reader's Perspective at Chapter End:**
The reader understands they're reading an adoption story with racial and religious complexity (Jewish mother, Black father, Black adoptive parents, 1964 Detroit). They've met baby Derrick and his new family. The "Congratulations" sign has been introduced as a recurring symbol.

**What Keeps Reader Engaged:**

- The promise: "That journey was just beginning"—clear signal more is coming

**Open Questions:**

- Who was Burton, the Black law student birth father?
- How will being "between worlds" affect Derrick's childhood?
- What does "chosen" vs. "belonging" mean in practice?
- Will Derrick ever understand why he was congratulated?

**Potential Confusion:**
- Timeline: Is this a flashback structure or chronological? (Unclear if narrator is looking back or this is happening in real time)
- Birth mother's identity: She's unnamed, which is realistic but might make her harder to track

**Potential Boredom:**
- **Low risk in Chapter 1**—birth/adoption stories are inherently engaging
- Some readers might want faster pacing to Derrick's conscious experiences

**Narrative Hook Strength:** **STRONG** (8/10)
The setup is compelling, emotionally resonant, and promises a complex journey. The "Congratulations" sign is an excellent recurring motif.

## CHAPTER 3: Lessons from Aunt Mae

**Reader's Perspective at Chapter End:**
Reader understands that Aunt Mae's house provided unconditional belonging that contrasted with the more conditional love at home. - Aunt Mae's house as refuge creates empathy for young Derrick

**Open Questions:**

- Will he seek Aunt Mae-style acceptance elsewhere?
- What happens when he can't find that unconditional space?
- Is Mom's conditional love intentional or just her personality?
- Does Aunt Mae know how much these visits mean to him?

**Potential Confusion:**
- **Still very early childhood**: We're now three chapters in and still in elementary years—readers might wonder when we'll get to adult Derrick
- **Timeline still vague**: "1960s-1970s" doesn't anchor us precisely
- **Aunt Mae's role**: The "surrogate mother to her two sisters" is mentioned but could be clearer about family structure

**Potential Boredom:**
- **LOW-MODERATE RISK**: This chapter has more specific scenes than Chapters 1-2

- But we're still in pattern of "here's another formative childhood experience"
- Readers might still wonder: "When does the plot start? When does something happen?"

The painful goodbye and guilt ("Was I ungrateful?") add emotional complexity. But it's still setup rather than conflict, and readers are waiting for dramatic incident.

- Aunt Mae's direct speech: "What do you want to do today, Shug?"
- Physical moments: hands covering his on the ice cream crank, cool hand on forehead
- The ending guilt/longing creates emotional tension

**WHAT COULD STRENGTHEN IT**:
- More specific age/year to ground timeline
- Clearer sense of how often these visits happened and over what time span
